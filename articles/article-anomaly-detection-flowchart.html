Anomaly Detection Flowchart: The Three-Layer Detection System Guide (2026)

Master every detection method — visual checks, CCTV monitoring, and paperwork verification — with our step-by-step flowchart and comparison tables.

Strategy Guide
July 12, 2026 | 12 min read

Every failed shift in Animal Hospital (Anomaly) traces back to one root cause: you missed an anomaly. The game doesn't punish you for being slow — it punishes you for being wrong. A single overlooked mismatched eye, a CCTV figure you shrugged off, or a paperwork discrepancy you didn't catch can cascade into a sanity death spiral that ends your run in seconds.

This anomaly detection flowchart guide breaks down the entire detection system into three distinct layers — Visual Inspection, CCTV Monitoring, and Paperwork Verification — and gives you a repeatable, step-by-step process to catch every anomaly type before it catches you. Whether you're a new player learning your first tells or a veteran optimizing Shift 5 runs, this flowchart will make your detection near-perfect.

The Three Detection Layers — Overview

Animal Hospital Anomaly uses a layered detection system. Each layer catches different anomaly types, and no single layer covers everything. You need all three working together to achieve consistent clears. Here's how they compare:

Layer What It Catches Timing Skill Floor Anomaly Types Covered
Visual Inspection Physical appearance tells When patient arrives at room Low-Medium ~12 types (eyes, teeth, face, body, twitching)
CCTV Monitoring Behavioral anomalies in hallways Continuous (between treatments) Medium ~6 types (staring, figures, movement, hiding)
Paperwork Verification Data mismatches on patient files At check-in desk / clipboard Medium-High ~5 types (photos, appointments, name/record errors)

The key insight most beginners miss: these layers are not optional alternatives — they're sequential checkpoints. The optimal workflow runs all three in order. Skipping paperwork because you "already checked the eyes" is how Skinwalkers slip through.

Layer 1: Visual Inspection — The First Line of Defense

Visual anomalies are the most common and the easiest to spot once you know what to look for. When a patient enters a treatment room, you have a brief window to inspect their physical appearance before beginning the treatment workflow. Always look before you treat.

The visual layer covers the largest number of anomaly types. These include:

  • Eye anomalies: Three eyes, hollow face, wide eyes with smile, mismatched eyes with sharp teeth, censored/pixelated eyes, different-colored eyes, and the classic "staring" tell
  • Body anomalies: Unnatural body proportions, void/empty body silhouette, twitching/jerking movements, head-banging behavior
  • Mouth/teeth: Human teeth on animal patients (one of the subtle tells that catches beginners off-guard)
  • Ear anomalies: Different ear shapes or sizes compared to the patient's species profile
  • The Skinwalker: The most dangerous visual anomaly — looks perfectly normal but is revealed during Fire in Room events or through specific item interactions

Visual Check Flowchart (do this for EVERY patient):

  1. Patient enters room → Stop. Don't start treatment yet.
  2. Look at the face: eyes normal? Two eyes, same color, no pixelation? → Pass
  3. Look at the mouth: teeth match the species? No human teeth on animals? → Pass
  4. Look at the ears: same shape/size on both sides? → Pass
  5. Look at the body: proportions natural? No void/empty sections? → Pass
  6. Movement: any twitching, jerking, or head-banging? → Pass
  7. All 5 checks pass? → Safe to treat. Proceed to Layer 3 (Paperwork).
  8. Any check fails? → This patient is anomalous. Do NOT treat. Use the appropriate counter-item or move to the next patient.

Layer 2: CCTV Monitoring — The Hallway Watch

CCTV anomalies don't appear in the treatment room — they appear in the hallways and camera feeds while you're doing other tasks. This layer requires a completely different mindset: instead of looking at patients, you're looking at screens and corridors for signs that something is wrong somewhere in the hospital.

CCTV-detectable anomalies include:

  • Camera Figure: A shadowy figure visible on CCTV that shouldn't be there — approaches rooms and can trigger hostile events
  • Staring at Cameras: A patient or entity that turns to face the camera directly — a clear hostile signal
  • Hiders: Anomalies that lurk in unoccupied rooms and ambush you when you enter — only visible on CCTV as movement in empty rooms
  • Stalker: An entity that follows your character through hallways — visible on CCTV as a trailing figure
  • Slime: Traces of an anomaly's movement visible as residue on camera feeds
  • Ghost: A translucent figure that flickers across camera views — indicates a Death Ritual may be incoming

CCTV Check Routine (run this between EVERY patient treatment):

  1. Finish treating (or rejecting) a patient → Before walking to the next room, check CCTV.
  2. Scan each camera feed: any figures in empty hallways? Any patients staring at cameras? Any movement in unoccupied rooms?
  3. Check for Slime trails or Ghost flicker — both are precursors to larger events.
  4. If you spot a Camera Figure or Stalker → Equip your defensive item (Taser/Fire Extinguisher) before moving.
  5. If you spot Hiders in an empty room → Avoid that room entirely or enter prepared with a counter-item.
  6. All clear → Proceed to next patient.

CCTV discipline is what separates Shift 3 players from Shift 5 players. Beginners tend to tunnel-vision on patient treatment and forget to check cameras. Make the CCTV sweep a muscle-memory reflex — it costs 3 seconds and saves runs.

Layer 3: Paperwork Verification — The Final Gate

The paperwork layer is the most overlooked detection method, and it's where the game hides its most devious anomalies. When you check in a patient at the front desk, their file appears on the clipboard or computer screen. This file can contain mismatches that indicate an anomaly — and some anomaly types can ONLY be caught through paperwork.

Paperwork-detectable anomalies include:

  • Incorrect Photo: The photo on file doesn't match the patient who arrived — a classic identity swap
  • Static Photo: The photo appears frozen or distorted — indicates a mimic or disguised entity
  • Unnatural Photo: The photo itself contains visual anomalies (distorted features, extra eyes in the photo)
  • Cursed Photo: The photo has an eerie quality — looking at it too long can drain sanity
  • Missing Appointment: The patient has no scheduled appointment — they showed up uninvited

Paperwork Check Flowchart (do this AT CHECK-IN):

  1. Patient arrives at check-in → Pull up their file before sending them to a room.
  2. Check the photo: does it match the patient standing in front of you? Same species, same features? → Pass
  3. Check the photo quality: is it clear, natural, and static? No distortion, pixelation, or uncanny elements? → Pass
  4. Check the appointment log: is this patient actually scheduled? Does the time slot make sense? → Pass
  5. Check the name/record: does the name match the species? No obvious mismatches (e.g., "Cat" label on a dog)? → Pass
  6. All checks pass? → Send to room. Proceed to Layer 1 (Visual) when they arrive.
  7. Any check fails? → Do NOT send to room. This is an anomaly — reject or use the appropriate counter.

The paperwork layer is especially critical for catching Mimic Patients — entities that look visually identical to real patients but have telltale signs in their file. If you skip paperwork, Mimics will walk right past you.

Complete Detection Flowchart — The Full Loop

Here's the complete three-layer detection loop that you should run for every single patient, every single shift:

Step Action Layer Time Cost What You Catch
1 Patient arrives at front desk — 0s —
2 Pull up paperwork, check photo + appointment Paperwork 2-3s Mimic, Missing Appointment, Photo anomalies
3 Paperwork clean? → Send to room — 1s —
4 Visual inspection in room (face, teeth, ears, body, movement) Visual 2-4s Eye anomalies, body deformations, twitching, human teeth
5 Visual clean? → Begin treatment — — —
6 Treatment complete → CCTV sweep before next patient CCTV 2-3s Camera figures, hiders, stalkers, ghosts, slime
7 CCTV clear? → Go to Step 1 for next patient — — —

Total added time per patient: ~5-8 seconds. That's nothing. And it's the difference between a clean Shift 5 clear and a sanity death spiral at Shift 2.

Detection Speed vs Accuracy — The Tradeoff

There's a real tension in Animal Hospital Anomaly between speed and thoroughness. Run too fast, and you miss tells. Run too slow, and you can't complete the patient queue before the shift timer expires. The flowchart above adds ~5-8 seconds per patient — but can you afford that time?

The answer depends on your class choice:

  • Surgeon — Speed boost on cure compensates for the time spent checking. Net zero time cost.
  • Paramedic — Large Speed Cola lets you rush between rooms, bank time for checks.
  • Head Nurse — Extra inventory slots mean less time juggling items = more time for checks.
  • Intern — No speed bonuses. You WILL feel the time pressure. Prioritize the Paperwork layer (fastest, highest-impact) and do quick Visual glances.

For the DNA Analysis shop upgrade: this upgrade speeds up patient identification at the check-in desk, effectively reducing the Paperwork layer's time cost to near zero. It's one of the best upgrades for detection-focused players.

Advanced Detection: The Triple-Check Protocol

For Shift 4-5 and beyond, where anomaly density spikes and emergencies overlap, we recommend the Triple-Check Protocol. This is the same flowchart as above but with additional safety margins:

  1. Check 1 (Paperwork): At desk — verify photo, name, species, appointment. Flag any mismatch.
  2. Check 2 (Visual): In room — full 5-point inspection (eyes, teeth, ears, body, movement). Even if paperwork passed.
  3. Check 3 (CCTV): After treatment — sweep ALL cameras, not just the hallway outside your current room.

The Triple-Check Protocol is especially important against the Skinwalker, which can pass Visual and Paperwork checks but is revealed during Fire-in-Room events visible on CCTV. If you've been following the standard flowchart, the Skinwalker is the one anomaly that can still slip through — the Triple-Check catches it.

Multiplayer Detection — Team Layer Assignments

In multiplayer, the three-layer system becomes a team coordination tool. Instead of every player running the full loop, assign layers:

  • Player 1 (Front Desk): Handles Paperwork layer exclusively. Sits at check-in, flags every mismatch before patients enter rooms.
  • Player 2 (Room Handler): Handles Visual layer. Receives patients from Player 1, does the 5-point inspection, and treats clean patients.
  • Player 3 (Camera Operator): Handles CCTV layer. Watches camera feeds full-time, calls out threats to the team.
  • Player 4 (Floater/Fighter): Handles hostile encounters. Equipped with Taser/Fire Extinguisher, responds to CCTV callouts from Player 3.

This role-based approach is dramatically faster than having everyone do everything. For more multiplayer strategies, see our Multiplayer Team Roles Guide.

Detection Items — Tools That Help

Several items enhance your detection capabilities or compensate for missed checks:

  • Eye Drops: Reveals hidden visual anomalies that appear normal to the naked eye — a safety net for the Visual layer
  • Maple Syrup: Can be used on Bed Monsters hiding under beds — covers a CCTV blind spot
  • Fire Extinguisher: During Fire-in-Room events, used to reveal Skinwalkers — the only reliable Skinwalker detection method
  • Taser/X-Taser: Not a detection tool per se, but the instant-stun lets you survive a missed detection with minimal sanity loss

Practice Drills — Building Detection Muscle Memory

Detection is a skill, and skills need practice. Here's how to train:

  1. Shift 1 Drills: Run Shift 1 repeatedly but DO NOT treat any patient until you've completed all three layers. The low anomaly density gives you time to practice the full loop. Focus on accuracy, not speed.
  2. Paperwork Speed Drill: Stand at the check-in desk and practice pulling up files and comparing photos. Aim to identify mismatches within 2 seconds. This is the most time-efficient layer to master.
  3. CCTV Reflex Drill: After each patient, force yourself to check CCTV before moving. Time yourself — aim for under 3 seconds per sweep. Eventually, it becomes automatic.
  4. Shift 3 Stress Test: Run Shift 3 with the full Triple-Check Protocol. The higher anomaly density will pressure-test your detection loop under real conditions.

Within 10-15 runs of focused practice, the three-layer system will be burned into muscle memory. You'll start catching anomalies you didn't even consciously notice — your brain will flag "something's off" before you can articulate what.

Pro Tip: The single most impactful detection habit is the CCTV sweep between patients. Most anomalies that end runs are hallway threats (Hiders, Stalkers, Camera Figures) — not room anomalies. If you only adopt one habit from this guide, make it the 3-second camera check after every treatment.
